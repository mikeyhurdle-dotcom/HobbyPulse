Strategic question being answered: **content is cheap to generate, so what makes top sites in these niches sticky? What voice patterns and value-exchange hooks bring readers back?** This document stress-tests the just-drafted voice rules against the patterns that actually make sticky sites sticky, and proposes retention plays that are Hawk-doable (i.e. don't require Mikey to become a personality).

## 1. Per-site briefs

### Tabletop

#### Shut Up & Sit Down (gold standard)
- **Voice signature.** Comedy-essay critic voice. The board game is a vehicle for an argument, joke, or feeling — never a spec sheet. Quentin Smith and Tom Brewster have distinct, named voices that disagree on camera. The narrator is always *someone* with a stance, never a brand.
- **Forbidden tone.** Press-release neutrality. They never say "this is a great game for groups who enjoy strategic depth" — they'd rather be wrong than bland.
- **Trade-off honesty.** Brutal. Quinns has called BGG top-100 darlings "boring" and "a waste of an evening" on camera. They gave *Twilight Imperium* a recommendation that doubled as a warning about your weekend.
- **Personality cue.** Named writers, recurring jokes (the "GAMES NEWS" jingle, the recurring rivalry between reviewers), running personas. The reviewer IS the brand.
- **Retention mechanism.** (1) Weekly podcast cadence. (2) The annual "Top 50 of all time" list — updated each year, generates argument and re-engagement. (3) A shop with curated picks (their taste IS the product). (4) Patreon community (paid loyalty layer). (5) Long enough video runtime that fans schedule it like a TV show.
- **Value exchange.** Reader gives time + money (Patreon, shop). Gets entertainment + curated taste. Ads exist but are not the primary play.

#### Dicebreaker
- **Voice signature.** Editorial games-journalism. Mid-tier editorialised but still recognisably journo-house — bylines matter, takes are allowed, but the voice is more "reasonable enthusiast" than "comedian critic".
- **Forbidden tone.** They mostly avoid spreadsheet-of-features posts; they prefer feature angles ("why X game's expansion misses the point").
- **Trade-off honesty.** Decent. They'll say something is overrated, but more cautiously than SU&SD.
- **Personality cue.** Bylines (Wheels, Snowman, Maddie etc. on the sister Eurogamer-network sites). Named writers with consistent beats.
- **Retention mechanism.** (1) News cadence — board game industry news is their bread. (2) Reviews pegged to release dates (reader returns to check before buying). (3) Editorial features ("The 25 best…" updated lists). (4) Newsletter. (5) Network-effect with Eurogamer audience.
- **Value exchange.** Reader gives email + ad attention. Gets news + reviews.

#### The Dice Tower
- **Voice signature.** Tom Vasel-driven, even on the website. Avuncular, generous, plays-everything, more "celebrate the hobby" than "tear it down".
- **Forbidden tone.** They actively avoid contrarian takedowns. The brand is positivity (sometimes to a fault — flaw-spotting is weak).
- **Trade-off honesty.** Mediocre by design. Tom rarely says a game is bad, more often "not for me". The audience knows this and reads accordingly.
- **Personality cue.** Tom Vasel's face/voice. The brand and the man are inseparable.
- **Retention mechanism.** (1) Daily YouTube cadence (massive volume). (2) The annual *Dice Tower Awards* — community ritual. (3) The cruise (literal IRL community). (4) "Top 100 of all time" lists ranked by Tom personally — endlessly debated. (5) Personality loyalty: people watch for Tom, not the games.
- **Value exchange.** Attention, ad revenue, occasional Patreon, big Kickstarter pledge drives. Trust is the currency.

#### Polygon (tabletop section)
- **Voice signature.** Mainstream-pop-culture voice applied to tabletop. Stronger on D&D/RPG news and "what's hot in 2026" than deep board game crit.
- **Forbidden tone.** Hobbyist-only inside-baseball. They keep it accessible.
- **Trade-off honesty.** Light. They're a network site; they don't want to alienate.
- **Personality cue.** Section editors but not strongly. Polygon-house voice.
- **Retention mechanism.** Network site; readers come for everything else and find tabletop on the way through. Not a destination on its own.
- **Value exchange.** Ads + network trust. Low individual loyalty per article.

#### BoardGameGeek (the value-exchange exemplar)
- **Voice signature.** No editorial voice — community generates the content. Forum is the product.
- **Trade-off honesty.** Crowdsourced. The aggregate weight + user reviews are the verdict.
- **Personality cue.** None at the platform level; user reputation is the layer.
- **Retention mechanism.** **The strongest in tabletop.** (1) Personal collection database — users build their library on BGG and won't leave because their data lives there. (2) The Hotness (real-time community signal). (3) Forum threads (rules clarifications, designer Q&A). (4) The annual rankings (constantly contested). (5) GeekGold (gamification). (6) GeekMarket (P2P selling).
- **Value exchange.** Reader gives data (their collection, their plays, their ratings, their reviews). Gets the network effect — every game has 10,000 reviews because everyone contributes. **This is the sticky-site bullseye:** the user's own data keeps them there.

### Sim-racing

#### Race Department / OverTake (same DNA, OverTake is the rebrand)
- **Voice signature.** Forum-house voice. News + community + mod downloads + reviews. The forum is the centre of gravity, not the editorial.
- **Forbidden tone.** They try to be neutral on hardware (ad-relationships) — and the audience knows. Reviews are mid-honesty; the forum threads carry the real verdict.
- **Trade-off honesty.** Editorial: medium. Forum: brutal — users will tell you a wheel base is bad in the threads when the review hedges.
- **Personality cue.** Named contributors, but the forum users are the personalities.
- **Retention mechanism.** (1) **Mods download library** — RaceDepartment's killer hook. You want a track for AC, you go there. (2) Forum (modders, league chatter). (3) News. (4) Free league sign-ups. (5) Hardware reviews are commodity content; the *moat* is the mod ecosystem.
- **Value exchange.** Reader downloads mods (gives email). Joins forum (gives presence). Hosts/runs leagues (gives content). Gets a habitat.

#### traxion.gg
- **Voice signature.** Younger, esports-leaning, performance-focused. F1 and esports-coverage forward.
- **Forbidden tone.** They avoid "sim racing as a hobbyist gear obsession" — they're more "sim racing as competition".
- **Trade-off honesty.** Decent but not their primary axis. They cover esports results more than gear takedowns.
- **Personality cue.** Named writers, esports angle.
- **Retention mechanism.** (1) Esports calendar coverage (results, schedules). (2) F1/series news pegged to real-world race weekends. (3) Competition coverage as recurring ritual.
- **Value exchange.** Calendar utility + race-weekend ritual. Not deep gear loyalty.

#### Boosted Media
- **Voice signature.** Pure-personality. Laurence (Boosted) and the team have named, recognisable voices. YouTube-first; the site is a footer to the videos.
- **Forbidden tone.** Press-release voice. They will absolutely call a wheel base bad on camera.
- **Trade-off honesty.** Strong — gear reviews include "skip if" calls and "this firmware is broken right now" notes.
- **Personality cue.** Named hosts, recurring bits, a recognisable production aesthetic.
- **Retention mechanism.** (1) YouTube subscription (the main retention). (2) Patreon community. (3) "Best wheel base for £X" updated lists. (4) Personality loyalty.
- **Value exchange.** Time + Patreon + ad attention. Pure personality model.

#### GTPlanet
- **Voice signature.** Long-running forum-and-news site. Veterans-of-the-hobby voice. Strong Gran Turismo lineage.
- **Forbidden tone.** They lean traditional — not edgy, not contrarian.
- **Trade-off honesty.** Light editorial; community is honest.
- **Personality cue.** Forum culture more than named writers.
- **Retention mechanism.** (1) Long-tenure forum (some users 15+ years). (2) GT-series release-date calendar (20+ years of news cycles). (3) Community leagues. (4) Habit/ritual — readers have been visiting for a decade.
- **Value exchange.** Forum presence + memory-of-the-hobby. Hard to bootstrap (impossible to fake 20 years of culture).

### Newsletter / sticky-format references

#### First (the morning briefing newsletter)
- **Voice signature.** Lord-tier curation: a single editor's daily picks, hand-selected, with a voice. Newsletter as point of view, not aggregator.
- **Retention mechanism.** Daily email cadence + curated quality. Reader trusts the editor's filter. Open-rate is high because the editor doesn't waste your time.

- **Value exchange.** Email + paid sub. Frameworks-as-product.

- **Retention mechanism.** Daily 6am cadence. Predictable structure (top story, market wrap, around the web, breakroom). Brand-funny tone.
- **Value exchange.** Email attention. Brand-as-friendly-filter.

## 2. Cross-cutting patterns — what makes sticky sites sticky

### Voice patterns that recur across the sticky tier

1. **Named opinion-holder voice.** Every sticky site has at least one recognisable narrator: Quinns, Tom, Laurence, Ben Thompson. The reader returns *to a person*, not to a brand. The flat-earth corollary: brand-only voices (network sites without bylines, AI-generated copy) have the worst retention by far. The reader feels nothing for "Polygon Tabletop". They feel something for "Quinns".
2. **Willingness to call things bad.** SU&SD trashed *Twilight Imperium* without apologising. Boosted will say "the firmware on this base is currently broken, don't buy it this month". Forum voices on RaceDepartment will tell you the truth the editorial can't. **The flinch from bad reviews is the AI-tell at site level.** Sites that hedge to protect ad relationships build no loyalty; readers unconsciously discount their recs.
3. **Specific-numbers-as-substance.** Stratechery names companies. Boosted names torque, polling rates, firmware versions. SU&SD names designers, durations, table configurations. The reader tests the writer's authority by pattern-matching specifics they already know. **Pass that test once and they trust you for the next 10 articles.**
4. **A consistent, repeatable frame.** Stratechery's "aggregation theory". SU&SD's annual top 50. Tom Vasel's "dice tower top 100". The sticky site has a *named recurring artefact* the audience expects to be updated. The artefact is the retention hook even when the weekly content is forgettable.

### Retention mechanisms that recur most often

1. **A weekly ritual.** First (daily). Stratechery (Monday + paid weekly). Morning Brew (daily 6am). SU&SD (Friday podcast + Monday news). The reader's calendar gets a slot for you. No ritual = no slot = no retention.
2. **A ranked list that updates.** SU&SD top 50, Tom Vasel's top 100, Boosted's "best wheel base under £X" list, Stratechery's frameworks restated. **The list is the most replayable retention asset in this category.** Readers come back to argue with it, share it, watch it shift.
3. **A calendar / release-date hook.** GTPlanet's GT release cycle. traxion.gg's race-weekend coverage. Dicebreaker's pre-release reviews. iRacing season schedules. Sim racing has Friday deals + new release dates; tabletop has Spiel des Jahres + Essen + new expansions. **Calendar pegging gives the reader a reason to check this week and not next.**
4. **A community surface where users contribute.** BGG's collection database is the textbook case — the user's own data anchors them. RaceDepartment's mods + forum. GTPlanet's 20-year forum. The user becomes part of the system; leaving costs them their data, their reputation, or their friends. **This is the deepest moat in the category by far.**
5. **Personality loyalty.** Quinns, Tom, Laurence, Ben Thompson. The reader returns for the writer. Hard for Hawk; possible for the *site* if a recurring "voice" is constructed (named persona, consistent frame).

### What's missing from the new voice rules (gap analysis)

The drafted voice rules are excellent at the **phrase + paragraph level**: banned phrases, sentence rhythm, "skip if", concrete numbers. They block the AI-tells beautifully.

What they don't do — and what the audit confirms is Hawk's actual problem — is force *retention shape* at the article and site level:

- **No retention scaffolding.** The voice rules don't ask "why does this article make the reader come back next Tuesday?" Every Hawk evergreen lives alone; there's no recurring artefact.
- **No named narrator.** SU&SD has Quinns. Boosted has Laurence. The voice rules describe *a tone* but not *a narrator*. AI defaults to brand-voice; without a named persona to anchor opinion, even good prose reads anonymous.
- **No structural-diversity rule that maps to retention.** The audit caught the 9-step skeleton; the voice rules don't yet have a "post-shape diversity" clause. Sticky sites have *recognisable shapes* (the SU&SD review video format, the Stratechery Monday essay), but they vary the shapes within the constraint. Hawk does the inverse — same shape every time, no recognisable shape that earns retention.
- **No artefact requirement.** Sticky sites have a top-50 list, a release calendar, a weekly deals roundup, a tracked spec table. The voice rules don't yet demand "this article points to or updates a recurring artefact".
- **No value-exchange clause.** The voice rules don't ask the reader to give anything (email, account, contribution). The site is pure pull-based, which means retention depends entirely on Google + memory. Both fail at scale.

## 3. Recommendations for TabletopWatch retention

Three to five recommendations, ordered by Hawk-doability and impact:

### 1. The TabletopWatch Top 50 — updated quarterly (Hawk-doable, high impact)
Pick 50 board games, rank them, *publish the ranking*, and re-publish every quarter with movement annotations ("Spirit Island held #2; Brass: Birmingham fell to #4 after the Nov reprint pricing settled"). The list is the recurring artefact. Hawk can absolutely generate the rationale per game; Mikey only needs to lock the ordering once and approve quarterly updates. **The list is the SEO play AND the retention play AND the newsletter hook.** SU&SD and Tom Vasel both ride this artefact for 90% of their stickiness; it's not a coincidence.

### 2. A weekly Friday deals newsletter (Hawk-doable, high impact)
Hawk already runs the deals scrape. Wrap it in a Friday email. Hook: "the 5 best UK board game deals this week, ranked by % off RRP, with skip-if notes". One curated voice, not a list. Newsletter sub becomes the value-exchange asset. Gives the site an email list — the most defensible retention surface in 2026, period. The deals roundup post (the audit's keep-tier example) is the prototype; turn it into a Friday email cadence.

### 3. A Spiel des Jahres / Essen calendar (Hawk-doable, medium impact)
Pin a "what's coming in tabletop 2026" page that updates as releases land. Link every upcoming release to its expected RRP and a "track this" mailing-list signup (price-alert-shaped). **This is calendar-pegging done as a retention surface.** The reader bookmarks it; the email captures lead.

### 4. A named narrator persona (medium-doable, very high impact)
The voice rules describe a tone but not a named narrator. Give TabletopWatch a recurring persona — a fictional but consistent reviewer with a name, a Kallax photo, a weekly-game-group reference (the partner who plays 30-min games but not 3-hour ones is already half-built into the voice rules). Sign every evergreen post with the persona. Use the same persona in the newsletter. **Reader loyalty is loyalty to a person; brand loyalty is rare.** Hawk's writes-by-default voice can be a named character; the named character ages much better than anonymous house voice.

### 5. The "What this replaces in your collection" widget (Hawk-doable, low effort, distinctive)
Pull from the voice rules' "mention what the game replaces" pattern and turn it into a structured field on every game review: `replaces: Splendor, Azul`. Display as a recurring card across the site. Readers who own a game land on it and get told "if you have this, you can skip X" — the most actionable thing TabletopWatch can do that BGG doesn't. **Compounds with Top 50 ranking — every game has 'replaces' annotations and 'sits next to' annotations.** Becomes the editorial database BGG forum threads can't replicate.

### Recommended NOT to chase

- A forum. Cannot bootstrap one in 2026; BGG owns this surface and 25 years of community gravity.
- A Patreon. No personality, no Patreon — wait until persona has audience.
- A paid sub. Nothing currently differentiated enough to charge for.

## 4. Recommendations for SimRaceWatch retention

### 1. The Friday deals roundup as newsletter (Hawk-doable, high impact)
Same play as TabletopWatch — the existing deals roundup is the prototype. SimRaceWatch deals are arguably *more* sticky than tabletop because hardware shoppers wait for sales and budget six months in advance. **A Friday email with "this week's best UK sim hardware deals, ranked by % off RRP" lands harder in this niche than tabletop.** GPL Racing + Demon Tweeks + MOZA-direct + Trak Racer prices + a one-line skip-if for each. Email list = retention.

### 2. A maintained "best wheel base under £X" tier list (Hawk-doable, very high impact)
Boosted Media owns this surface for video; nobody owns it well in written form. Hawk can build a tiered, price-banded comparison: "best base under £200 / £400 / £700 / £1500" updated as new firmware drops, new SKUs land, prices shift. Each tier names the winner, the runner-up, the upgrade target, and a "wait if" annotation. **This is the SEO play AND the retention artefact AND the affiliate-conversion surface.** Update quarterly minimum, faster on firmware/release events. This list becomes the canonical destination for "I have £X to spend" Google traffic — and *every* update gives readers a reason to return.

### 3. A release / firmware calendar (Hawk-doable, medium impact)
Sim racing has a steady drumbeat of firmware releases (MOZA Pit House, Fanalab, Asetek), software updates (ACC patches, iRacing seasons, EA WRC content drops), and new SKUs. Most of these are scattered across forums and Discords. **Aggregate them onto one calendar page that updates weekly.** This is mod-database-shaped without needing the mods themselves — the *index* is the value. Readers bookmark it; mailing list captures it.

### 4. Named narrator + rig-photo (medium-doable, very high impact)
Same logic as TabletopWatch. A recurring rig-owner persona (the voice rules already describe one — "the most opinionated DD-base owner in the league chat"). Pin the persona's rig photo somewhere visible. Sign every gear review with the persona. **The persona's *spec history* — what they upgraded from, what they're saving for — is the editorial spine.** Boosted's whole audience returns for "Laurence's takes"; SimRaceWatch can do the same with a written persona at lower cost. The voice rules are 80% of the way there; what they're missing is naming the narrator.

### 5. A "diminishing-returns calculator" tool (medium-doable, distinctive)
The voice rules already contain the editorial spine for this: pedals → base → rim → seat → motion. Build a simple tool: reader enters their current setup + their budget, the tool tells them where the next £X should go. Output is opinionated, not balanced. **This is a tool that NOBODY in sim racing has done well — and it's directly aligned with the site's voice.** Tool retention beats article retention every time; tools also generate inbound links. The tool reads off the same recurring artefact as the tier list and the newsletter (consistent rankings = consistent answers).

### Recommended NOT to chase

- A mods library. RaceDepartment owns this and the technical lift is huge.
- A forum. Same reason as tabletop.
- League hosting. SRO and existing leagues own it; hosting is a logistics business.

## 5. Cross-site retention plays Hawk can run

Not site-specific:

1. **Email capture on every list-post.** "Want to know when this list updates? One email per quarter, no spam." This single line below every Top 50 / Tier list captures the retention asset for free.
2. **`replaces` and `sits next to` structured fields** on every product mention — feeds into BGG-style network effects without needing a forum.
3. **A "last updated" date prominently shown** on every artefact. Reader returns when the date moves. (BGG, Tom Kuhn's wirecutter, every sticky list-site does this.)
4. **A monthly Mikey-voiced editor's note on the homepage**, even if Hawk drafts it. One paragraph. Anchors the persona; signals "a human is here". Inoculates against "AI-generated site" pattern recognition.

## 6. Voice-rule additions to suggest

These should be added to BOTH `tabletopwatch-voice.md` and `simracewatch-voice.md`, adapted per site.

### Add to "Required vibes" section

**Named narrator anchor.** Every evergreen post is signed by the same recurring persona. The persona has: a real-feeling rig / Kallax / game group, a known bias (e.g. "I'm a heavy-euro skeptic", "I'm an ACC GT3 driver, I underweight rally"), and a continuity (last week they upgraded X; this week they're testing Y). **Hawk: stop signing posts as anonymous house voice. Pick a name and stick to it.**

**Recurring-artefact anchor.** Every evergreen post should reference, link to, or feed into a recurring artefact: the Top 50 (tabletop) or the tier list / calendar (sim racing). If the post can't be plausibly threaded into a recurring artefact, ask whether it should exist as a standalone evergreen at all.

**Reader-action ask.** Every evergreen post ends with a single ask the reader can complete in one click: subscribe to the deals newsletter, sign up for a price alert on a named product, see the updated tier list. **Not a CTA paragraph — one line, one ask.** No ask = no retention surface for that reader.

### Add to "Forbidden structures" section

**No anonymous house voice.** Reject any post that doesn't carry the named-narrator persona signal somewhere in the first three paragraphs (a first-person rig/group reference, a continuity reference, a known-bias acknowledgement).

**No artefact orphans.** Reject any evergreen that doesn't link to or feed at least one recurring artefact (Top 50, tier list, deals page, calendar, replaces-graph). Standalone evergreens with no ecosystem hook are retention dead-ends.

### Add to "Pre-publish voice QA"

- [ ] Post is signed by the named narrator persona, with at least one continuity / first-person cue in the first three paragraphs
- [ ] Post links to or feeds into at least one recurring artefact (Top 50, tier list, deals roundup, calendar)
- [ ] Post ends with a one-line reader-action ask (newsletter, price alert, tier list)
- [ ] Closer is the most useful concrete sentence AND the action-ask is separate from it (don't conflate the two)

### Add to "AI-tell-specific blocks"

**Anonymous-brand voice.** AI defaults to "this article will explore". Real enthusiasts default to "I". If three paragraphs in a row contain no first-person, no rig/group reference, and no continuity — the persona has dropped out and the post reads brand-anonymous. Rewrite.

**Standalone-evergreen drift.** AI loves to write self-contained articles that explain everything from scratch. Sticky sites assume the reader has read the Top 50 already. Reference it. Compress. The reader doing 12 visits to TabletopWatch this year should feel each post building on the last; the new reader can click through to the artefact for context.

## 7. Final framing

The voice rules block AI-shaped *prose*. The audit caught AI-shaped *structure*. This analysis catches AI-shaped *site architecture* — every Hawk evergreen lives alone, signed by no-one, leading nowhere. The fix isn't more posts. The fix is:

1. A named narrator
2. A recurring artefact (Top 50 for tabletop; tier list + calendar for sim racing)
3. A weekly Friday newsletter pulling from the deals scrape Hawk already runs
4. A one-line reader-action ask on every evergreen

These four moves — none of which require Mikey to become a personality, all of which Hawk can execute given the right scaffolding — would put TabletopWatch and SimRaceWatch in the same retention shape as the sticky tier of competitors. Without them, even perfect prose decays; readers drop in from Google once and never return.
