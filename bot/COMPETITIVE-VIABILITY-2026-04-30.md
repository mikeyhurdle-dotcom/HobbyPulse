Owner: Mikey (via Claude Opus 4.7, 1M context)

Question being answered: **is the AI-augmented, always-on, mascot-led content site model actually viable in 2026, or is HobbyPulse chasing a dead category?**

---

## TL;DR — the answer

**Minimal-mode it. Don't kill, don't commit.** The category isn't dead, but the "AI-runs-it / Mikey-does-5-hr/week / £500-mo" formula has effectively zero clean public success cases in 2026 — every site at that revenue tier has either a named human voice, a forum/community moat, or 5+ years of domain authority on an established site (none of which HobbyPulse has). The maths still works for ONE site (TabletopWatch, where the audience-vs-incumbents gap is wider) at very low investment (~3-5 hr/week) over 9-12 months. SimRaceWatch is the weak leg — Boosted/RaceDepartment/Traxion own the niche, the affiliate margins are thin (~10% on Moza, undisclosed and likely lower on Demon Tweeks), and you're a UK-first written site against video-first incumbents. Run TTW lean on a Friday-newsletter + quarterly-Top-50 cadence with the "Hawk" persona; quietly let SRW go (or fold its deals into TTW under a cross-vertical "deals" flag). Reallocate the 60-80% of HobbyPulse time you free up back into the iOS hobby-tracker apps where the StoreKit cycle is actually closing.

## The category state

**The 2024-2025-2026 HCU collision was real, was bigger than the category admits, and is permanent.**

- **HouseFresh (the canonical case)**: lost 91% of Google traffic after Google's March 2024 core update on top of an earlier 95% drop in September 2023, going from ~4,000 daily visitors to ~200. Recovery only landed October 2025 — TWO YEARS later — and required: continuous site improvements, a YouTube channel, a collaboration with Linus Tech Tips, AND favourable subsequent algorithm updates. ([ppc.land](https://ppc.land/housefresh-achieves-notable-traffic-recovery-after-google-algorithm-impacts-2/))
- **Reviewed.com / Forbes Advisor / CNN Underscored**: hit by the same wave; the HCU specifically punished "affiliate review content with no clear human expertise". CNET's AI-content rollback was a *pre*-HCU warning shot — Washington Post called the program a "journalistic disaster" and forced corrections on dozens of finance articles. CNET only recovered after rebuilding human editorial review on top.
- **Net effect**: by mid-2024, Search Engine Land documented a 91% Google-traffic collapse for one small affiliate review site that was outranked by sites bolting AI-content onto pre-existing domain authority. ([searchengineland.com](https://searchengineland.com/review-site-google-traffic-affiliate-seo-content-440143))

**What's still working in 2026:**

- Recovery patterns are clear and require what HobbyPulse currently does NOT have: extensive personal testing documented with photos/video, original methodology, content consolidation (less content, not more), real first-person experience. ([almcorp.com](https://almcorp.com/blog/google-december-2025-core-update-complete-analysis-recovery-guide/))
- The December 2025 HCU shifted Google's evaluation **from content quality to content necessity** — i.e. "does this article need to exist if the better one already does?" New entrants without differentiation lose by default.
- Sites that recovered did so via the playbook the voice-retention doc already names: named expertise, consolidation, first-person rigs / collections / hands.

**What this means for HobbyPulse specifically:** the audit-confirmed problems (no first-person, no named narrator, abstraction-laundering, fake-data padding, generic Amazon search URLs) are the *exact* signals the December 2025 HCU was tuned to penalise. Even if Hawk's prose passes the banned-phrase filter, the structural slop is what gets the site flagged. **The current site shape is the worst possible shape for late-2026 Google.**

## Mascot-led content — when it works

**Two hard rules emerge from the comp scan:**

1. **The mascot needs to ride a product or person, not a blog.** Every successful brand mascot anchors to something the user touches: Duolingo's Duo lives on a streak/practice app where the mascot's "judgmental nag" is the product mechanic ([adweek](https://www.adweek.com/brand-marketing/duolingo-duo-owl-marketing-strategy/) — 47.7M DAUs, 41% rev growth, 20M+ social followers, ALL on the back of Duo as personality wrapper). Mailchimp's Freddie sat on top of an email tool. Slack's Slackbot is the onboarding voice of the product itself. Mailmunch / Headspace / Bombas — same pattern. **None of the famous mascot wins are content-site mascots.**
2. **The mascot needs creative-team velocity behind it.** Duolingo's social team is 2 FTE + a few contractors with minimal approval friction — that's why Duo can be unhinged on TikTok. Tale of Painters added "Topsy" as a mascot in 2023 only after **12 years** of named-author content (Garfy, Stahly etc.) had already built the audience — Topsy is merch-and-flavour on top of a real human team, not a load-bearing personality. ([taleofpainters.com](https://taleofpainters.com/2023/11/news-our-first-merch-collection-and-new-mascot/))

**Where it goes cringe (and HobbyPulse is currently in this zone):**

- **Mascot-as-content-author** without a real human voice underneath reads as childish or condescending. Clippy is the canonical fail (Microsoft over-deployed an "assistant" with no real value, became annoying within weeks). Mr. Mucus polled at 50% disliked.
- **Brand-mascot-without-velocity**: most B2B SaaS mascots (Drift's bot, the 5-mascot rebrand HobbyPulse has) don't move the needle because nobody updates them with a daily/weekly personality beat. The mascot becomes a logo, not a character.
- **Mascot-as-AI-ghostwriter-cover** is the riskiest pattern — you're effectively saying "the AI is a character" and readers reverse-engineer it within 3-5 articles. The voice-retention audit already caught this: signing every Hawk post with an anonymous house voice is what makes the site read AI-anonymous.

**What's transferable to HobbyPulse (if you go minimal):**

- **One persona per site, named, with a continuity** (rig spec history for SRW, Kallax photo for TTW). The voice-retention doc already drafted this — it's the right move.
- **The persona signs the newsletter** weekly, not just the blog. That's where Duo-style "personality with a cadence" actually compounds.
- **The persona has a known bias** (Quinns hates 4-hour Euros; Laurence hates Logitech). Bias is what makes the recommendation feel like a recommendation, not an SEO listicle.

**What's NOT transferable:**

- Multi-mascot rebrands. The current 5-mascot setup (per `bot/REBRAND-MASCOTS.md` reference) is a brand-design exercise, not a retention asset. Pick one. Maybe two (one per site). Kill the rest.
- TikTok-style mascot virality. That is a 2-FTE-with-creative-velocity job, not a Mikey-side-project job. Don't even try.

## Realistic economics

**Display ad revenue tier requirements (2026 numbers):**

| Tier | Network | Min sessions/month | RPM range |
|---|---|---|---|
| Entry | Google AdSense (current) | None, but unusable revenue at <50k pageviews | $1–$5 typical |
| Journey by Mediavine | Mediavine "Journey" | **1,000 sessions/mo** | similar to AdSense early on, scales with growth |
| Raptive (was AdThrive) | Raptive | **25,000 pageviews/mo, 50%+ from US/UK/CA/AU/NZ** | $33–$44 average ([thisweekinblogging](https://thisweekinblogging.com/mediavine-vs-raptive/)) |
| Mediavine "Official" | Mediavine | $5,000+ annual revenue threshold | $22–$43 average 2024-2025 |

**Implied: HobbyPulse needs ~25,000 monthly pageviews on at least one site to access Raptive economics.** At an average $35 RPM, 25k pv = ~$875/mo (~£700/mo), so display-ad alone *can* hit £500/mo per site if you cross the Raptive bar. Below that bar, AdSense yields are 5-10x lower and the £500 target becomes affiliate-dominant.

**Affiliate commission realities (2026):**

- **Amazon Associates UK**: 1-10% across categories. Toys & Games is ~3%. Sports & Outdoors ~3%. **Amazon cut kitchen commissions to ~3% post-HCU; the trajectory is downward across the board.** ([newormedia](https://newormedia.com/blog/best-website-niches-for-affiliate-marketing-in-2025/))
- **Moza Racing**: 10% to influencers ([zleague.gg](https://www.zleague.gg/theportal/the-controversial-moza-affiliate-program-sim-racing-upgrade-dilemma/)) — this is the best public sim-racing rate in the niche.
- **Demon Tweeks**: undisclosed; pattern-match suggests 3-6% on motorsport/gear.
- **eBay Partner Network (rover)**: 1-4% depending on category and buyer type.
- **Goblin Gaming / Element Games / Wayland (board games UK)**: typically 5-10% on niche board game retailers — but the volume is small.

**What it takes to hit revenue tiers (synthesised from Indie Hackers / Failory / Fat Stacks income reports):**

| Target | Time-to-rev (new domain, no existing audience) | Hours/wk to maintain | Investment shape |
|---|---|---|---|
| £100/mo | 8-14 months | 5-10 hr/wk | 30-50 quality articles + email list 500-1k |
| £500/mo | 14-24 months | 10-15 hr/wk | 100-200 articles + 25k+ pv/mo + email list 2-5k |
| £2k/mo | 24-48 months | 15-25 hr/wk + outsourced help | 300+ articles + 100k+ pv/mo + multiple revenue streams + named voice |

**Critical caveat**: those numbers are *pre-HCU*. Post-2024-HCU, every income-report blog quietly notes "but new domains are harder now" — the 8-14 month curve is widening to 12-18 months for new domains in 2026. ([nicheinvestor](https://nicheinvestor.com/are-niche-sites-still-profitable/))

**HobbyPulse's actual position right now (pulled from audit):**

- TTW: 8 visible blog posts, ~22k product listings, 0 ads serving (AdSense under review), ~unknown but small monthly traffic (no GA4 wired, can't measure).
- SRW: same shape.
- **Probability of hitting Raptive's 25k pageview bar within 6 months: <10%** based on the 1.74% of new pages ranking top 10 within a year. ([ahrefs](https://ahrefs.com/blog/how-long-does-it-take-to-rank-in-google-and-how-old-are-top-ranking-pages/))
- **Probability of hitting £500/mo combined within 6 months: <5%** given current state.
- **Probability of hitting £500/mo combined within 12 months WITH the voice-retention overhaul shipped: ~20-30%** — and that's optimistic.

**Compared to small iOS app economics:**

- **Mewstro / hobby-tracker family**: a niche tracker can charge 30-60% higher subscription rates than generic apps; one quit-smoking solo-dev case pulled $700 LTV in year 1 with 8 products (so ~$87.50 per product avg) — but the *focused* app outperformed the diversified portfolio. Niche tracker apps are described as "fast to build, easy to explain, reliable earners". ([medium.com — Eddie Larsen](https://medium.com/@e2larsen/niche-tracker-apps-build-fast-charge-monthly-d51427aa035b))
- **Cycle time**: 2-4 weeks to ship a new tracker app off the Mewstro framework. StoreKit revenue starts on day 1 of approval (vs 6-12 months on a content site).
- **"Reliable earner" tier**: based on indie iOS revenue patterns, a single hobby-tracker at 100-300 paid subs at £3-5/mo = £300-£1500/mo. **Plausibly the same revenue as HobbyPulse in 1/3 the time.**

**The honest comparison: every hour spent on HobbyPulse is competing with an hour spent shipping the next Sketchi / Hushlo / Spindl / Bouldy / Rollbarra / Purrouette improvement.** The opportunity cost is real and large.

## HobbyPulse's specific niche-fit

### Board games (TabletopWatch)

- **BoardGameGeek owns the database moat**: 25+ years, user collections, GeekGold, forum, GeekMarket. Cannot be displaced. ([boardgamegeek thread on the SU&SD effect](https://boardgamegeek.com/thread/3267435/measuring-the-shut-up-and-sit-down-effect))
- **SU&SD owns the comedic-critic voice**: Quinns + Brewster, weekly cadence, annual top 50 list, Patreon. The audience knows the voice and demands it. New entrants compete *with* the voice, not against the spec sheet.
- **The Dice Tower owns positivity / volume**: Tom Vasel's daily YouTube + cruise + awards. Personality-loyalty is locked.
- **Dicebreaker owns editorial mid-tier news**.
- **Tale of Painters owns hobbyist UK Warhammer**: 14 years deep, named authors, Patreon, merch via Topsy mascot. ([taleofpainters.com](https://taleofpainters.com/about-the-painters/))

**Where there IS still an unfilled niche**: UK-specific *deals* discovery. SU&SD doesn't do real-time price tracking; BGG doesn't surface UK retailer offers; Tale of Painters covers gear but not deals at the deal-aggregator level. **TabletopWatch's deals scrape (22,686 listings) IS a real differentiator if the editorial layer matches it.** This is the only realistic moat.

**Affiliate margin reality**: board game commissions in the UK average 3-7% (Amazon UK at 3% for Toys/Games, 5-10% for indie retailers like Goblin Gaming). On a £40 average board game order, that's £1.20-£4 per converted click. To hit £500/mo affiliate-only on board games at a 4% blended rate: **you need ~£12,500/mo in tracked GMV, which at a 3% click-to-buy rate requires ~50,000 outbound affiliate clicks per month**. That's a lot of pageviews.

### Sim racing (SimRaceWatch)

- **Race Department / OverTake owns mods + forum** — irreplaceable, 15+ years.
- **Boosted Media owns hardware-review video** — Laurence is the voice; written competitors lose by default.
- **Traxion owns esports-and-results** — calendar-pegged retention.
- **GTPlanet owns franchise-hardcore** — 20+ year forum.

**Where there IS still an unfilled niche**: written, opinionated tier lists with UK pricing. Boosted does video; nobody owns the written equivalent. The voice-retention doc's "best wheel base under £X tier list" is the right play. But **affiliate margins are even thinner here** — Moza's 10% is the best public number, most retailers are 3-5%. On a £400 wheel base at 5% = £20 per converted click. £500/mo = ~25 converted sales = ~2,500 clicks of buying intent.

**The UK-first angle is real but small**: Boosted is UK-based already. Any UK pricing advantage is marginal.

### Combined: room for another UK-first written-content site?

**Honestly: no, not for SimRaceWatch.** The competitive surface is owned and the affiliate margins don't justify the SEO climb against established video-first incumbents who already do UK pricing.

**Marginal yes for TabletopWatch**, *only* if it leans hard into the deals-aggregator + UK-specific angle that BGG/SU&SD/Dicebreaker actively don't do. The blog content is table-stakes; the deals data is the moat.

## The honest "always-on AI content" answer

**Has anyone publicly built a clean "AI runs the content / owner does ~5 hr/week / £500+/mo" content business in 2025-2026?**

**No clean cases.** I searched specifically for this pattern across Indie Hackers, Failory, Fat Stacks, niche-affiliate forums, and Substack disclosures.

What you find instead:

1. **AI-augmented sites with named human editors**, where a real human does 10-20 hr/wk of editorial work and AI does drafting. These DO hit £500-£5k/mo, but the human-hour floor is non-negligible. Not "set it and forget it".
2. **Pure-AI content farms**, which without exception have either (a) been deindexed by 2024-2025 HCU updates, (b) been quietly sold off as the founder lost interest, or (c) are still alive but at sub-£100/mo despite massive content volume.
3. **AI-content sites built on existing domain authority**, where a 10-year-old site bolts AI on. These work but require the pre-existing authority. ([Search Engine Land documented these specifically](https://searchengineland.com/review-site-google-traffic-affiliate-seo-content-440143) — established sites outranked HouseFresh's expert content with bolted-on AI articles.)
4. **Newsletter-as-content-vehicle wins** (Pragmatic Engineer at $1.5M/yr, Stratechery at 40k+ subs, Morning Brew acquired by Insider). All have **named human voices**, none are AI-runs-it.
5. **Niche affiliate sites in the £100-£500/mo band** that survived HCU all share these traits: the owner has photos/videos with the products, has a name and shows up in YouTube comments / forums, has been at it 3-5 years. None are 5-hour-a-week operations.

**The closest pattern that works**: solo creator with one product (newsletter or app), uses AI for drafting + research, ships ~3-5 hr/wk of polished output under their own name. Revenue accrues to the personal brand. **This is closer to the iOS hobby-tracker model than to HobbyPulse.**

**Direct answer to Mikey's question**: the always-on AI content site at £500/mo is a unicorn pattern. The "AI handles scale + human provides judgment" pattern works but requires the human hours. The "Hawk does it all, Mikey checks in weekly" model — there's no public success case at the £500+/mo tier in 2026 that I could verify.
