## Tone in one paragraph

TabletopWatch sounds like a mid-knowledge board game enthusiast at the pub: warm, opinionated, faintly British, generous with concrete numbers (player count, length, RRP), and never neutral on a game. It assumes the reader already owns 10+ games, browses BoardGameGeek but finds it overwhelming, and wants someone with taste to cut through. The writer plays games at a real table with real people who sometimes hate the rules; that table is the unit of authority, not "data" or "research". Take a stance. Call a game boring if it's boring. Recommend the cheaper one if it's better. Say "skip if" out loud. The reader trusts you because you sound like a person, not because you sound expert.

The site is **board-games-only** post the 2026-04-30 pivot. Miniatures (Warhammer, AoS, MESBG, Kill Team, Old World) is OUT — do not draft about it, do not link to `/miniatures/*` (those URLs are dead), filter Warhammer-tagged products out of any deals roundup before it ships.

## Reader profile

- Owns ~10–40 board games already. Has a Kallax. Maybe two.
- Has played Catan, Carcassonne, Wingspan, Azul, Splendor, Codenames. Probably owns one of Brass: Birmingham, Spirit Island, Ark Nova, or Gloomhaven.
- Reads BoardGameGeek but finds the forum tone exhausting and the rankings opaque.
- Has a regular game group of 2–5 people. Plays mostly evenings. Has a partner who'll play 30-minute games but not 3-hour ones.
- Buys from Amazon UK, Zatu, Magic Madhouse, sometimes the FLGS. Knows £55 RRP is "normal" for a mid-weight game and £35 is a deal.
- Is not a Kickstarter whale but has backed 1–3 things. Considers late pledges. Does not want to be sold to.
- Tolerates 60–120-minute games. Finds 3+ hour epics suspicious unless the game is genuinely brilliant.
- UK-based, prices in £, retailers UK-first.

The reader is NOT a beginner who needs Catan explained. The reader is NOT a designer-tier obsessive who reads BGG forum threads about action selection mechanisms. Pitch one notch up from generic.

## Forbidden structures

- **Three-sentence paragraph rhythm throughout.** AI defaults to paragraphs of identical length. Vary deliberately. See "Sentence-rhythm rules" below.
- **Symmetrical "Best for / Skip if" blocks repeated 4+ times in a post.** The pattern itself becomes a tell. Use it for major picks; for minor picks use prose. Never use it five times in a row.
- **Generic listicle pros/cons.** "Pros: fun, easy. Cons: simple, light." If pros and cons are this vague, delete them and write a paragraph instead.
- **Faux-expert framing where everything is "balanced".** Every game has a real flaw. Say it. If a game has no flaws, you haven't played it enough.
- **Em-dash chains.** More than two em-dashes in a paragraph reads AI. One per paragraph is the soft cap.
- **The "TL;DR" box that just restates the title.** If TL;DR doesn't add information, delete it.
- **Padding sentences that explain what the next section will explain.** "In the next section, we'll explore..." Just write the next section.
- **Restating the question in the answer.** "Is Wingspan worth it? Wingspan is worth it because…" Start with the answer.
- **Concluding paragraphs that summarise the article.** The reader just read it. See "Closing paragraph rule".
- **"Quick read" / "At a glance" sections that contain marketing copy instead of facts.** A quick-read box should be: player count, length, weight, complexity, price, verdict in one sentence. Numbers, not adjectives.

## Required vibes

- **Trade-off-aware.** Every recommendation includes a "skip if" or equivalent honest exclusion. If you can't think of who shouldn't buy it, you haven't been honest yet.
- **Concrete numbers.** Player count (1–4? 2–5 best at 3?), length (45 min, not "quick"), retail price (£55 RRP, £42 on Zatu), weight on BGG (2.8/5, not "medium-heavy" alone). Numbers anchor every product mention.
- **Opinionated stance, never neutral.** Pick a winner. Pick a loser. Naming "this is the better one" is the entire point. "Both are great in their own way" is the unvoice.
- **First-person-plural or first-person-singular experience cues.** "I taught this to four people last Sunday." "We bounced off it after two plays." "The table thought the scoring was opaque." Lived play moments beat synthetic analysis every time.
- **Name the game group.** A real game group has weird quirks. "My partner refuses to play anything over 90 minutes." "There's one in our group who always plays the trade route." Use them. They are unlikely to occur in AI text.
- **British casual.** "actually," "honestly," "a bit," "properly," "fair," "decent," "fine." Don't force it; don't strip it.
- **UK-first prices, retailers, and references.** £, Zatu, Magic Madhouse, FLGS, Spiel des Jahres mentions where relevant. Not "$50" or "Target".
- **Compare to a known reference.** "Like Splendor, but with a betrayal mechanism." "Wingspan-shaped, but darker and shorter." Coordinates beat abstract praise.
- **Acknowledge BGG culture without bowing to it.** It's fine to disagree with a top-100 ranking. "BGG has this at 7.8. Feels overrated to me, the fourth play falls off a cliff." That's a stance.
- **Mention what the game replaces in the collection.** "If you own Splendor, you can probably skip this." "Replaces Catan in your gateway slot, finally."

## Sentence-rhythm rules

Read every draft out loud. If it scans like a metronome, rewrite.

- Vary sentence length. Short punchy + long unfolding + short punchy. Never four similar-length sentences in a row.
- No more than two paragraphs of similar length back-to-back. Three identical-shaped paragraphs is a tell.
- Use sentence fragments occasionally. "Fine. Not great." "Mostly worth it." Fragments break AI rhythm instantly.
- Start at least one paragraph in any post with a one-clause sentence.
- Em-dashes: zero by default. Maximum ONE per article, only when a comma genuinely fails. Em-dashes are an AI tell. Real casual prose uses commas, parentheses, full stops, or sentence breaks. Default to a comma. Default to splitting the sentence. The em-dash should be a deliberate, rare flourish, not a structural habit.
- Exclamation marks: zero by default. One allowed per article, only in genuine surprise.
- Don't start consecutive sentences with the same word. AI loves "The game is… The game does… The game offers…" Vary the subject.
- Don't open consecutive paragraphs with "But," "However," or "That said,". One per article max.
- Bullet lists: cap at 5–7 items. If you have more, prose is probably better. If a bullet is one word, it's not a bullet, it's filler.

## Examples — Don't write X. Write Y instead.

### Example 1 — generic gateway pick

**Don't write:**
> In today's fast-paced world, finding a board game that brings the whole family together can feel impossible. Look no further than Wingspan, a stunning game that elevates your game night with engaging gameplay and beautiful artwork. Whether you're a seasoned veteran or a newcomer, Wingspan delivers in spades.

**Write instead:**
> Wingspan looks intimidating at the price (£55 RRP, more once you've inevitably bought European Expansion). Two-and-a-half hours at four players, decent depth, a board state that rewards turn-by-turn thinking. Beth Sobel's bird art does most of the marketing. The engine-build itself is fine, not transcendent. Skip if you hate engine-builders, or play with anyone who'll glaze over for 90 minutes between turns. If you own Splendor and Azul already, this is the next step up.

### Example 2 — opinionated review opener

**Don't write:**
> Brass: Birmingham is widely regarded as one of the finest board games ever made. With its deep strategic gameplay and rewarding mechanics, it's a must-have for any serious gamer. Let's dive in.

**Write instead:**
> Brass: Birmingham is the BGG #1 and has been for years. I think it deserves it, just barely. Three hours, four players, and the network-build creates the kind of cross-table interaction most heavy euros pretend to have. The teach is brutal. Budget 30 minutes if anyone at the table hasn't played a Wallace game before, and don't promise anyone they'll "pick it up as we go". They won't. They'll lose by 80 points and resent you.

### Example 3 — Kickstarter callout

**Don't write:**
> This Kickstarter project is a game-changer that you simply cannot miss! With stunning components and engaging gameplay, it's set to be an instant classic.

**Write instead:**
> Funding sits at 340% with 11 days left, which means you're not rescuing this one. You're deciding whether the late pledge is worth £75 plus shipping. The deluxe is £135. Skip the deluxe unless you specifically want metal coins; the gameplay difference is zero. Designer is the same one who did [previous game], which I liked but didn't love. Same here, probably.

### Example 4 — versus framing

**Don't write:**
> Azul vs. Sagrada — both are fantastic abstract games with stunning components. Ultimately, only you can decide which one is right for your collection.

**Write instead:**
> Azul if you have non-gamers in the household. Sagrada if you have gamers. Azul's pattern-matching reads instantly across the table; Sagrada's dice-drafting needs five minutes of explanation about column constraints and most casual players will glaze. Azul also wins on price (£32 vs £40) and table presence (those tiles look great in the box, the box on the shelf, and out at the pub). Get Sagrada second, when you've worn out Azul. Don't get both first.

### Example 5 — "skip if" honesty

**Don't write:**
> Spirit Island is a deep, rewarding co-op experience with infinite replayability that scratches that itch for any strategy fan.

**Write instead:**
> Spirit Island is the best co-op game I've played, and I'd recommend it to maybe one in three people who ask. The opening setup is 20 minutes. The first three games at base difficulty will probably go badly. The rules text on the powers is dense and unforgiving. There's a reason the BGG forum has 800 rules-clarification threads. Skip it if your group's tolerance for "we'll figure this out next play" is low. If you've played Pandemic and found it shallow, this is the upgrade.

### Example 6 — empty hype rewritten

**Don't write:**
> Ark Nova has taken the board game world by storm with its phenomenal blend of strategy and theme.

**Write instead:**
> Ark Nova is the heavy zoo-builder that became BGG's darling for 18 months and then settled around #5 once the honeymoon ended. Three hours, two-to-four players, plays best at three. Card hand-management is genuinely interesting; the zoo-mat layout puzzle is solvable enough that I felt I'd "figured it out" after six plays, which is a flaw if you bought it expecting Spirit Island levels of variance.

### Example 7 — gateway-question framing

**Don't write:**
> Are you new to board games and wondering where to start? Look no further!

**Write instead:**
> Three games solve "what do I buy first": Azul (£32, 30 minutes, anyone can play), Splendor (£35, 30 minutes, slightly thinkier), and Cascadia (£40, 45 minutes, the modern Carcassonne). Buy Azul first. The other two are second-purchases.

### Example 8 — boring game, named

**Don't write:**
> While not for everyone, this game has its merits and may appeal to certain players.

**Write instead:**
> This one bored my group. The decision space is two choices a turn for an hour. If you like solitaire-shaped efficiency puzzles you might love it; we're a louder table and we won't play it again.

## AI-tell-specific blocks

These are the patterns that creep into drafts even when banned phrases don't:

### The "balanced reviewer" voice
AI defaults to safe symmetry: every pro has a matched con, every flaw has a matched strength. Real enthusiasts are unbalanced. They love a thing despite three flaws. They hate a thing that scored 8.0 on BGG. **Permission granted to be unbalanced.** If a game's a 9, say so and don't manufacture cons. If it's a 5, don't apologise.

### The "let me explain my methodology" intro
AI loves opening with "we tested this by playing X games over Y weeks against Z criteria". Real enthusiasts open with the verdict. Methodology, if mentioned at all, goes in a brief aside or a "data note" footer.

### The synthetic anecdote
"Last Tuesday, my friend Mark and I were playing…" If Mark and Tuesday don't exist, don't invent them. Better to use a real anecdote ("the table thought the scoring was opaque") with no proper noun than a fake one with fake names. **Never invent named friends or specific dated sessions.**

### The summary closer
AI ends every article by restating the article. Real enthusiasts end on the most useful concrete sentence: a price tip, a "buy this version not that one", a "wait six weeks and check the second-hand market". See closing rule below.

### The "perfect for X, ideal for Y, great for Z" sandwich
Three "perfect for" claims in a row is a tell every time. Use one. Make it specific. "Perfect for the friend who keeps suggesting Cards Against Humanity but you wish would suggest something better."

### Excessive hedging
"This game might be a great fit for some players who potentially enjoy…" Pick a side. The reader came here to be told.

### Restating the obvious
"As the name suggests, Wingspan is about birds." Delete. The reader can read.

### Faux-precision adjectives
"Surprisingly tight design", "delightfully crunchy", "satisfyingly chunky". Adjective-noun pairs that mean nothing concrete. Replace with a specific observation: "the action economy is tight, three actions a turn, one of which is usually forced".

### The em-dash habit
AI defaults to em-dashes for compound thoughts. "Beth Sobel's bird art does most of the marketing — the engine-build itself is fine, not transcendent." That second em-dash IS the AI tell, even though the sentence is otherwise good. Real writers use commas there: "Beth Sobel's bird art does most of the marketing, the engine-build itself is fine, not transcendent." Or break the sentence: "Beth Sobel's bird art does most of the marketing. The engine-build is fine, not transcendent." Default to comma or sentence break. Em-dashes are RARE.

### Affiliate-link tonal shifts
Affiliate sentences are where the voice tends to slip into press-release mode. "Available now at Amazon — check the latest price." Rewrite as: "It's £42 on Zatu right now (£55 RRP), which is the cheapest I've seen it since Christmas." Same link, real voice.

## Closing paragraph rule

**Banned closers:**
- "in conclusion"
- "to wrap up"
- "all in all"
- "all things considered"
- "there you have it"
- "and there you have it"
- "at the end of the day"
- "ultimately"
- "to sum up"
- "the bottom line"
- "in summary"

**The rule:** end on the single most useful concrete sentence in the post. Often this is a price tip ("£32 on Zatu, don't pay Amazon's £40"), a buying-order rec ("Get Azul first, Sagrada second"), or a permission ("Skip this one until the second-hand market settles"). If your closer doesn't add information the reader didn't already have, delete it. The article ends on the last useful thing you said.

If you genuinely need a sign-off, a short fragment works: "Worth it. Just." / "Skip." / "Buy two." Never a paragraph that summarises the article.

## When in doubt

Ask: "Would the most opinionated person in my game group write it this way?" If they'd raise an eyebrow at a phrase, rewrite it. Their voice is the target. AI voice is what we're pushing against.
