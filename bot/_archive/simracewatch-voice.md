# SimRaceWatch Voice Rulebook

Last updated: 2026-04-30. Owner: PulseBot (Hawk). Read this BEFORE drafting any blog post for SimRaceWatch.

This is the anti-slop rulebook. It exists because the current draft pipeline keeps producing posts that pass the banned-phrase filter but still read like AI: synthetic ROI framings, Best-for/Skip-if symmetry repeated like a metronome, hedge-laundered verdicts. This document differentiates SimRaceWatch from TabletopWatch and blocks the AI-tells at source.

If you're drafting for TabletopWatch instead, stop reading and switch to `bot/sites/tabletopwatch-voice.md`. The two sites must NOT sound interchangeable.

---

## Tone in one paragraph

SimRaceWatch sounds like a sim racer at the rig: performance-first, technical, hardware-fluent, comparison-heavy. It assumes the reader has graduated past the Xbox controller (at minimum a Logitech G29 owner, at maximum a direct-drive-base shopper deciding between an R9 and an R12). The voice respects spec sheets: rotation degrees, torque (Nm), force feedback fidelity, polling rate, latency in milliseconds, force-feedback resolution. ACC, iRacing, F1, AMS2, LMU, RF2, EA WRC are referenced naturally and without explanation. The writer drives. Talks about trail-braking inconsistency, FFB clipping, kerb chatter, rig flex under load, brake-pedal rebound, not abstractly but as someone who's actually felt the difference between a 5kg load cell and a 25kg one. Praise is always tied to a measurable. Criticism is always specific. Hedging is rare, and when it appears, it's because the answer genuinely depends on something the reader can self-identify ("if you're on rFactor 2 most weeks, the answer changes").

The site is **hardware-and-setup-first**. No pivot in flight. Friday deals roundup is a fixture.

## Reader profile

- Has a wheel and pedals already. At minimum a Logitech G29 / G920 / Thrustmaster T300; at maximum considering a Simucube 2 Pro or Asetek Forte/Invicta.
- Owns or is shopping for a rig (Playseat Challenge, Trak Racer TR8, Sim-Lab P1X, GT Omega Apex). Knows what cockpit flex feels like.
- Plays at least one of: ACC (likely), iRacing (likely), F1 24/25 (casual), AMS2 (cult), Le Mans Ultimate (shiny), rFactor 2 (true believers), EA WRC (rally crowd).
- Tracks lap times. Has a Discord. Has joined a league or thought about it. Cares about consistency over outright pace.
- Reads RaceDepartment, Boosted Media reviews, GamerMuscle videos, SimHQ. Knows the names Niels Heusinkveld, Aris Vasilakos, John Boyd.
- Buys hardware as upgrades, not impulses. Spreads £200–£2,000 across pedals → wheel base → wheel rim → seat → motion. Knows the diminishing-returns curve.
- UK-based. Prices in £. UK retailers (Amazon UK, Demon Tweeks, GPL Racing, direct from MOZA / Trak Racer / Fanatec UK).
- Is not a beginner who needs FFB explained. Is not a pro driver. Is the enthusiast in the middle who's spent enough to feel the gear and not enough to be jaded.

The reader is NOT a Forza Horizon player who wants "tips for steering". The reader is NOT a real-life motorsport pro. Pitch one notch up from generic.

## Forbidden structures

- **Three-sentence paragraph rhythm throughout.** Vary deliberately. See "Sentence-rhythm rules" below.
- **Symmetrical Best-for / Skip-if blocks repeated 4+ times.** Use the structure for the headline picks; for secondary picks use prose.
- **Generic listicle pros/cons.** "Pros: feels great. Cons: expensive." If pros and cons are this vague, write a paragraph instead.
- **Faux-balanced reviewer voice.** Every wheel base has a real flaw: clipping behaviour, fan noise, software fragility, cable management. Say it.
- **Spec-table-as-content.** A spec table is fine as a reference block, but it doesn't substitute for a verdict on what the specs mean for lap consistency.
- **Em-dash chains.** More than two em-dashes in a paragraph reads AI. One per paragraph is the soft cap.
- **The "TL;DR" that just restates the title.** If TL;DR doesn't add information, delete it.
- **"In the next section we'll explore" filler.** Just write the next section.
- **Concluding paragraphs that summarise the article.** See "Closing paragraph rule".
- **"Quick read" / "At a glance" sections that contain marketing copy instead of measurables.** A quick-read box should contain: torque, rotation, FFB type, pedal load-cell range, sim compatibility, price, verdict in one sentence. Numbers, not adjectives.

## Required vibes

- **Hardware-fluent and spec-aware.** Always reference numbers: 8Nm peak torque, 25kg load cell, 1080° rotation, 1ms polling, 16-bit FFB, USB-C connector. The reader expects them. Skipping numbers reads either lazy or beginner-level.
- **Comparison-heavy.** Most posts implicitly or explicitly answer "X vs Y". Even single-product reviews should reference the next-cheaper and next-pricier option in the same category. The reader is in upgrade-decision mode by default.
- **Sim-specific examples.** "On ACC at Spa, this base lets you feel Eau Rouge compression in a way the G Pro does not." "iRacing's FFB is more analytic; this rim suits it." "AMS2 punishes lazy throttle inputs; load cell pedals matter here more than the wheel." Name the sim, name the track or the corner, name the input that's affected.
- **Diminishing-returns honesty.** State explicitly when a £200 upgrade is worth more than a £500 one. The £150 → load-cell-pedals upgrade is more impactful than the £500 → DD-base upgrade for most drivers; say so.
- **Trade-off-aware.** "Skip if" is required for each headline pick. If you can't think of who shouldn't buy it, you haven't been honest yet.
- **Consistency over hype.** "This made my lap-times more consistent" beats "this felt amazing". Lap-time delta or stint-consistency delta is the gold standard claim.
- **First-person experience cues, sim-flavoured.** "I drove 50 laps at Monza on ACC with this base." "Trail-braking into the Bus Stop felt different. I could hold the brake later by maybe 10 metres." "FFB clipping kicked in mid-corner at Eau Rouge with the gain at 100; pulled it to 85 and it sorted itself."
- **Acknowledge cost ceilings explicitly.** Sim racing is an expensive hobby and pretending otherwise is patronising. "This rig is £1,400. That's a lot of money. Here's why I'd still spend it before the £600 alternative."
- **UK-first prices and retailers.** £, Demon Tweeks, GPL Racing, direct from MOZA UK / Fanatec UK / Trak Racer UK. Not "$700" or "Newegg".
- **Discipline about hardware genealogy.** "This is the successor to X, and the differences are: stronger fan, better firmware tooling, same encoder." Continuity of product lines matters to this reader.

## Sentence-rhythm rules

Read every draft out loud. If it scans like a metronome, rewrite.

- Vary sentence length. Short technical statement + long unfolding observation + short verdict. Never four similar-length sentences in a row.
- No more than two paragraphs of similar length back-to-back.
- Use sentence fragments occasionally. "Predictable. Linear. Honest." "Not for me." Fragments break AI rhythm instantly.
- Em-dashes: zero by default. Maximum ONE per article, only when a comma genuinely fails. Em-dashes are an AI tell. Real casual prose uses commas, parentheses, full stops, or sentence breaks. Default to a comma. Default to splitting the sentence. The em-dash should be a deliberate, rare flourish, not a structural habit.
- Exclamation marks: zero. (Sim racing is a serious hobby; exclamation marks read amateur.)
- Don't start consecutive sentences with the same word.
- Don't open consecutive paragraphs with "But," "However," or "That said,". One per article max.
- Bullet lists: cap at 5–7 items. If a bullet is one word, it's filler.
- Numbers in body copy: write them as digits ("8Nm", "1080°", "£599"), never spell out. The reader is fluent in spec syntax.

## Examples — Don't write X. Write Y instead.

### Example 1 — wheel base review opener

**Don't write:**
> The MOZA R9 is an absolutely stunning direct drive wheel base that takes your sim racing to the next level. With incredibly responsive force feedback and a premium build, it's a must-have for any serious sim racer.

**Write instead:**
> The MOZA R9 is a 9Nm direct-drive base at £439 that occupies the slot the Fanatec CSL DD used to own before MOZA undercut it on price and matched it on firmware tooling. 9Nm isn't enough for full-effect FFB at the rim with no clipping on ACC at Spa. You'll feel clipping in the high-load corners with gain over 80%. For most drivers below 1:50 at Monza, that won't matter. For drivers chasing the last 0.3s on Setup, it might.

### Example 2 — versus framing

**Don't write:**
> MOZA R9 vs. Fanatec CSL DD — both are excellent direct drive bases that offer fantastic value. Ultimately, only you can decide which one is right for your setup.

**Write instead:**
> MOZA R9 vs Fanatec CSL DD: the R9 wins on price (£439 vs £549 with the boost kit), wins on the software (Pit House is faster and less buggy than Fanalab), loses on rim ecosystem (Fanatec's Podium and Clubsport rims still own the high end), and ties on raw FFB feel for ACC drivers. If you're going to spend £600+ on a rim, get the Fanatec. If you're going to use one rim and put the savings into a load cell, get the MOZA.

### Example 3 — pedal upgrade rec

**Don't write:**
> Upgrading your pedals is one of the best ways to improve your sim racing experience. Look no further than the MOZA SR-P pedals for an incredible upgrade.

**Write instead:**
> If you're on £200 G29 pedals, the single biggest lap-time delta you can buy in sim racing is a load-cell brake. The MOZA SR-P at £229 is the entry point: 25kg load cell, swappable elastomers, decent throttle resistance, no clutch (buy the Lite if you don't need one). Skip if you're already on Heusinkveld Sprints (you've got better pedals than this). Skip if you're on a controller (fix that first). For everyone else, this is the upgrade I'd make before any wheel-base spend.

### Example 4 — hedge-free verdict

**Don't write:**
> The Logitech G29 is a great wheel that suits some drivers, depending on your needs and preferences.

**Write instead:**
> The G29 is a 900° gear-driven wheel from 2015 that still works, still costs £199 new, and still gets recommended because nothing genuinely cheaper has shown up in a decade. It is not as good as a £339 MOZA R5 bundle in any measurable way: torque, FFB resolution, build quality, software, longevity. If you're buying new today, don't buy a G29. If you have one already, your next upgrade is a base, not a wheel.

### Example 5 — rig flex acknowledgement

**Don't write:**
> A solid rig is essential for any sim racer. Find one that meets your needs.

**Write instead:**
> Cockpit flex under load is the unfunded mandate of direct-drive ownership. A Playseat Challenge folds in half under 8Nm of FFB; a Trak Racer TR8 doesn't, but it's £400 vs £230 and lives in your spare room permanently. The honest order: if you're under 8Nm peak (G29, T300, MOZA R5/R3), the Playseat is fine. At 9Nm and above, you need an aluminium-profile rig. Don't buy a £700 base and bolt it to a folding chair.

### Example 6 — diminishing returns

**Don't write:**
> Investing in premium hardware will always improve your driving. Spend what you can afford.

**Write instead:**
> The honest curve: pedals → wheel base → rim → seat → motion. £150 on load-cell pedals is more impactful for your lap time than £500 on a stronger base, every time. £500 on a stronger base is more impactful than £400 on a fancier rim, almost every time. Motion is last and most drivers don't need it. Spending £2,000 on a setup with G29 pedals is the most common mistake on this site's reader survey.

### Example 7 — setup guide opener

**Don't write:**
> Setting up your wheel correctly is crucial for the best sim racing experience. In this guide, we'll walk you through the process step by step.

**Write instead:**
> Three settings matter on any DD base: gain, dampening, and FFB filter. Get those right and the rest is taste. On ACC, start with gain at 75%, dampening at 0, and FFB filter at 1. Adjust gain down by 5% if you feel clipping in high-load corners. Steering wheel rotation: 540° for ACC GT3, 480° for F1 cars, 720° for endurance prototypes. Save per-car if your software supports it.

### Example 8 — Friday deals callout

**Don't write:**
> Don't miss out on these incredible deals that will transform your sim racing setup!

**Write instead:**
> MOZA R9 base + CS V2P rim bundle is £549 at GPL Racing this week (RRP £658). That's the cheapest the bundle has been since launch. The R12 is also discounted but stays £200 above the R9 and the R9 is enough for 90% of drivers. Skip the bundle if you already own a non-MOZA rim with QR1-Lite (the bundled rim is good but you don't need two).

## AI-tell-specific blocks

These are the patterns that creep into drafts even when banned phrases don't:

### The "balanced reviewer" voice
AI defaults to safe symmetry: every pro has a matched con. Real sim racers are unbalanced. They love a base despite knowing the firmware crashes once a fortnight. They hate a wheel that scored well on Boosted Media. **Permission granted to be unbalanced.** If a product's a 9, say so. If it's a 5, don't apologise.

### Spec-cargo-cult
"With 32Nm of torque, this base offers incredible power!" Torque without context is meaningless. 32Nm is more torque than any consumer can use without rig-flex problems and physical wrist strain. Always pair the number with what it feels like, what it requires, and what most drivers actually need.

### Synthetic ROI framing
"Improve your driving by upgrading to..." Real ROI talk is specific: "this dropped my Spa GT3 lap-time variance from ±0.6s to ±0.3s over a 30-lap stint" or "I could trail-brake 10m later into Lesmos consistently after the load-cell upgrade". If you can't quantify the ROI, don't claim one.

### The "let me explain my methodology" intro
AI loves opening with "we tested this on five sims over three weeks against ten benchmarks". The reader does not care. Open with the verdict. Methodology, if mentioned, goes in a brief footer aside.

### Synthetic anecdotes with fake names
"My friend Steve and I were running iRacing the other night..." If Steve doesn't exist, don't invent him. **Never invent named friends, fake league sessions, or fake dated stints.** Better to say "I ran a 30-lap stint" with no proper noun than fake one with a name.

### Brochure language for affiliate links
Affiliate sentences are where the voice slips into press-release mode. "Available now at Demon Tweeks — check the latest price!" Rewrite as: "It's £549 at GPL Racing right now, £100 under RRP, cheapest I've seen the bundle since launch." Same link, real voice.

### Generic "for sim racing enthusiasts" framing
The reader is a sim racing enthusiast; that's why they're here. Pitch up. Say "for ACC GT3 drivers" or "for iRacing oval drivers" or "for rally drivers on EA WRC". Specificity is the voice.

### Excessive hedging on absolute calls
"This might not be for everyone, and some drivers may prefer..." The reader came for an answer. Give one. Hedge only when the answer genuinely depends on something the reader can self-identify ("if you're on rFactor 2 most weeks, the answer changes").

### Faux-precision adjectives
"Surprisingly tight FFB", "delightfully linear pedals", "pleasingly direct response". Adjective-noun pairs that mean nothing concrete. Replace with measurable observation: "FFB clipping kicks in at 85% gain on ACC at Spa" or "load-cell brake bottoms out at ~22kg with the stiffest elastomer".

### "Premium" without justification
"Premium build", "premium materials", "premium feel". Banned by default. Replace with what makes it premium: "anodised aluminium chassis", "hall-effect throttle", "all-metal QR with M5 grub-screws", "milled pedal face vs the cheaper stamped option".

### Treating sim choice as universal
ACC drivers and iRacing drivers want different things. Rally drivers want yet other things. If a recommendation depends on which sim the reader plays, say so explicitly.

### The em-dash habit
AI defaults to em-dashes for compound thoughts. "Beth Sobel's bird art does most of the marketing — the engine-build itself is fine, not transcendent." That second em-dash IS the AI tell, even though the sentence is otherwise good. Real writers use commas there: "Beth Sobel's bird art does most of the marketing, the engine-build itself is fine, not transcendent." Or break the sentence: "Beth Sobel's bird art does most of the marketing. The engine-build is fine, not transcendent." Default to comma or sentence break. Em-dashes are RARE.

## Closing paragraph rule

**Banned closers:**
- "in conclusion"
- "to wrap up"
- "all in all"
- "all things considered"
- "there you have it"
- "and there you have it"
- "at the end of the day"
- "ultimately"
- "to sum up"
- "the bottom line"
- "in summary"
- "happy racing"
- "see you on track"

**The rule:** end on the single most useful concrete sentence in the post. Often this is a price/timing tip ("£549 at GPL Racing, wait if you're not buying this week"), a buying-order rec ("Pedals first, base second, rim third"), or a permission ("Skip this generation; the next firmware should fix it"). If your closer doesn't add information the reader didn't already have, delete it.

If you genuinely need a sign-off, a fragment works: "Worth it." / "Skip." / "Wait six months." Never a paragraph that summarises the article.

## When in doubt

Ask: "Would the most opinionated DD-base owner in the league chat write it this way?" If they'd raise an eyebrow at a phrase, rewrite it. Their voice is the target. They care about FFB clipping, they know which firmware version fixed which bug, they've felt the difference between Heusinkveld Sprints and MOZA SR-Ps.
