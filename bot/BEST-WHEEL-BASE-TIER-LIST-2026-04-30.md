*By Greg "Gilly" Gilmartin. Updated 2026-04-30.*

I sold my Caterham in 2018 because two kids and a Manchester mortgage made an £8k race-season impossible. Bought a £600 starter rig that month. Seven years later I'm three bases deep (G29, CSL Elite, MOZA R12) and the thing nobody on YouTube will tell you straight is that the £400-to-£800 jump buys you genuine FFB fidelity, and the £1,200-to-£2,000 jump mostly buys you headroom you'll never use and a fan you can hear during night stints. This list is built on that thesis. Not against it.

Boosted Media own this slot in video. RaceDepartment hedge half their gear writeups because the forum carries the real verdict in threads underneath. I'm trying for something neither of those does, which is a written tier list that picks a winner per band, names a base you should actively skip, and tells you where on the curve most readers should stop. I'm an ACC GT3 driver mainly, ex-real-track in MX-5 cup and Caterhams, currently on a Trak Racer TR8 in the spare room. Where my taste runs narrow (rally, oval) I say so and defer.

## At a glance, the one-line summary

Cheapest sane entry is a MOZA R5 bundle at £339. Sweet spot for 90% of readers is the MOZA R9 at £439, where FFB fidelity stops being the limiting factor and your pedals become the ceiling. Stop at £800 unless you specifically know what 15Nm gives you that 12Nm doesn't.

## Under £300, entry tier

**Best buy: nothing.** This is the dead-zone tier in 2026 and you should know it before you spend.

The Logitech G29 is still £199 new, which is the price it launched at in 2015. It works. It's gear-driven, 900 degrees, ~2.1Nm of torque, and the FFB resolution is poor enough that ACC trail-braking feel comes through as a vague rumble rather than the slip-angle signal you actually need. Cheapest Thrustmaster T128 is £179 with belt-and-gear hybrid drive, marginally better resolution, marginally worse pedal set. Both are fine if you're in for under £200 total and you've never tried better. Both are wildly outclassed by what £100 more buys you.

Skip pick at this tier: **Logitech G29 brand-new at full RRP**. Buying a brand-new G29 in 2026 is the most common mistake on this site's reader survey. If you must spend under £200, buy a used G29 for £80-£110 from CEX or Facebook Marketplace and save the rest for pedals. If you've got £250 in the budget, save up another £90 and skip this tier entirely.

What changes when you go up a tier: torque doubles, FFB becomes direct-drive (no gear noise, no detent), and you stop fighting the wheel for input it isn't giving you. ACC at Spa goes from "I think the rears are gone" to "I can feel the rears go".

Sim-specific: the G29 is fine for F1 25 casual drives because the assists are doing the work. It's bad for ACC because GT3 cars need slip-angle clarity. It's worse for AMS2 because AMS2's FFB is information-rich and the G29 can't carry it. For rally on EA WRC it's mediocre, gear noise plus low torque equals a base that talks more about itself than about the surface.

## £300 to £500, first DD or last great belt

**Best buy: MOZA R5 bundle (5.5Nm DD, ES rim, SR-P Lite pedals) at £339 from MOZA UK direct.**

The R5 bundle is the answer to "I have £350 and I want to do this properly". 5.5Nm of direct-drive torque is enough for ACC GT3 with gain at 75%, no clipping at Monza, mild clipping at Eau Rouge if you push to 90%. Pit House software is fast and reliable, USB-C connector, 16-bit FFB resolution. The included ES rim is plastic and not great, the SR-P Lite pedals are a load-cell brake at £339 all-in which is the bit that makes the bundle absurdly hard to beat. Nothing at this price gets you a load cell.

Runner-up: **Thrustmaster T818 at £429** for the rim-ecosystem play (Thrustmaster's TM ecosystem is locked but cheaper than Fanatec's), 10Nm peak torque on paper but the wheel-side ergonomics drag it down. Driver software is the same TM Control Panel that's been clunky since 2017. Buy if you already own TM rims. Don't buy if you don't.

Skip pick at this tier: **Logitech Pro Racing Wheel at £499**. Logitech finally shipped a DD base in 2022 and the price never came down to where it makes sense. 11Nm peak, but the rim is fixed, the FFB is sanitised compared to MOZA at the same price, and Logitech G HUB is a worse software experience than Pit House. If a YouTuber tells you this is "the safe choice" they have not actually compared it to a current MOZA R9.

Who should buy at this tier: G29/T300 owners ready to stop fighting their wheel, drivers who bought a second-hand rig and want one upgrade that moves the needle, anyone whose budget is genuinely capped at £400 all-in.

Who should NOT: anyone with £500+ available who's about to talk themselves into a "compromise". You're better off saving £100 more and buying R9 outright. The R5 to R9 jump is 5.5Nm to 9Nm of usable torque and the FFB filter quality steps with it.

What changes at the next tier: torque ceiling goes from "clips on Eau Rouge" to "clips only when you ask it to", the FFB filter on Pit House gets headroom, and rim-mounting gets serious (QR1-Lite vs full QR1).

Sim-specific: R5 is good for ACC, decent for iRacing GT3, fine for F1 25, slightly weak for EA WRC because rally needs faster direction-change response than 5.5Nm comfortably gives. AMS2 punishes the lack of headroom but the SR-P Lite pedals carry it.

## £500 to £800, the sweet spot for most readers

**Best buy: MOZA R9 at £439 base-only, or R9 + CS V2P rim bundle at £549 from GPL Racing.**

The R9 is the base I'd buy with my own money tomorrow if I had to start over. 9Nm peak torque, 16-bit FFB, USB-C, Pit House software, QR1 mount. On ACC at Spa with gain at 80% I get clean FFB without clipping through Eau Rouge and Pouhon. On iRacing it reads slip-angle cleanly enough that trail-braking into Turn 1 at Watkins Glen feels honest. On AMS2's rallycross layouts I get a kerb chatter signal that the R5 just smooths out. 9Nm is the figure where most cockpit rigs stop being the limiting factor (a Playseat Challenge folds at 8Nm under sustained load, a Trak Racer TR8 is fine).

Runner-up: **Fanatec CSL DD at £549 with the Boost Kit.** Same money as the R9 bundle, marginally sharper on cold-tyre feel through lap one and two, locked into the Fanatec rim ecosystem (which is good if you're going to buy a £600 Podium rim, neutral if you're not). Fanalab software is slower and crashes more often than Pit House (firmware 451 fixed the worst of it, still trails MOZA on stability). I owned the CSL DD for 18 months and swapped to MOZA. I would not swap back at this price.

Third place: **Simagic Alpha Mini at £539**, 10Nm peak, sharper on direction-change response than the R9 (Bryn the rally guy on this site rates it higher than I do for that reason), software is Simagic-direct and stable, ecosystem is thinner than MOZA or Fanatec. Buy if you mix circuit and rally. Skip if you're ACC-only because the R9's smoother high-load feel suits long stints better.

Skip pick at this tier: **Thrustmaster T818 with the TM Open Wheel rim at £699**. The base is fine. The all-in cost climbs over £700 once you spec a usable rim, at which point you should buy the R9 bundle and pocket the difference.

Who should buy: anyone who's spent two seasons on a G29/T300 and knows they'll stay in the hobby, ACC league drivers who need consistency over a 30-lap stint, drivers stepping up from belt-drive who want headroom without overspending.

Who should NOT: drivers on Heusinkveld Sprints already (your pedals are better than your base would be at £600, and the pedals matter more), anyone whose actual rig is still a Playseat Challenge (you'll feel the cockpit fold before you feel the base's ceiling), iRacing oval-only drivers (different physics, different ranking, different list).

What changes at the next tier: the FFB ceiling stops being the limiting factor and your rig flex / pedals / rim become the visible weak link. The honest delta from R9 to R12 is 3Nm of headroom you'll only use on tracks where you shouldn't have it dialled up anyway.

Sim-specific: R9 is excellent for ACC, very good for iRacing road, very good for F1 25, good for AMS2, decent for EA WRC. CSL DD is marginally sharper for ACC at the cost of slower software. Alpha Mini is the rally pick at this tier (defer to Bryn).

## £800 to £1,200, hobbyist tier

**Best buy: MOZA R12 at £799 base-only, or R12 + KS rim bundle at £999.**

The R12 is the base I'm currently driving, and I'll be honest about why. I bought it because I wanted the headroom for league stints where I run 100% gain on cold tyres at Bathurst. I use that headroom maybe twice a season. The other 95% of my driving the R12 is not measurably different from the R9 because I'm not pushing it. 12Nm peak, 16-bit FFB, same Pit House software as the R9, same QR1 mount. If you're not sure why you'd want 12Nm over 9Nm, you don't want 12Nm over 9Nm.

Runner-up: **Asetek La Prima at £849 base-only.** 12Nm peak, Asetek's smoother torque ramp than MOZA's (genuine difference in long-stint consistency, I borrowed one for a fortnight), software is RaceHub which is stable and minimal, EU-aftercare track record is good (Anya the consumer-rights writer rates the warranty experience here, deferring to her on the warranty math). Rim ecosystem is thinner than MOZA's and twice the price of MOZA's at the high end.

Third place: **Fanatec CSL DD with the QR2 wheel-side at £899 all-in**, 8Nm without Boost Kit and the upgrade math gets murky. The CSL DD lives in this tier only if you're committed to the Fanatec rim ecosystem.

Skip pick at this tier: **the original Simucube 2 Sport at £1,099**, currently on sale at SimRacingBay. 17Nm peak which is more than you can use without flex problems, expensive software workflow (True Drive is powerful and configurable, also a faff), no companion pedal ecosystem. Simucube does fantastic high-end bases. The Sport is the awkward middle they've stopped iterating on.

Who should buy: drivers who know they'll be in the hobby for five-plus years, league drivers running 90+ minute stints where consistency matters, ex-real-track drivers chasing a specific feel they remember (which is what got me here, honestly).

Who should NOT: most readers. This is the tier where the diminishing-returns curve flattens hard. I owned the R9 before this and could not have told you in a blind test which was which after lap three of a clean Spa run.

What changes at the next tier: noise floor improves (the R12's fan is audible during night stints, the higher-end bases are quieter), magnetic encoders get higher resolution, software gets more configurable (which mostly means more time spent tweaking settings instead of driving).

Sim-specific: R12 is overkill for F1 25, well-matched to ACC league racing, useful headroom for iRacing endurance, mildly excessive for EA WRC unless you're running Welsh stages flat-out.

## £1,200 to £2,000, diminishing returns territory

**Best buy: Simucube 2 Pro at £1,299 base-only.**

This is the base I'd buy if I were doing it for hobbyist reasons rather than driving reasons. 25Nm peak (more than any cockpit rig under £1,000 can handle without flex), 22-bit encoder resolution, True Drive software is the sim-racing equivalent of a flight-sim throttle quadrant in terms of configuration depth. Granite Devices' two-year warranty is genuinely better than MOZA's one-year. The aftercare track record (deferring to Anya again) is excellent. The fan is quieter than the R12's at idle.

Honest read: I drove a Simucube 2 Pro for a week at a friend's rig and the FFB feel difference vs my R12 at Spa was real but small. Maybe 5% sharper on the curbing at Pouhon. Maybe 3% better at communicating tyre temperature changes through the rim. For £500 more. The drivers who can use that 5% are running league championships at the front. The drivers buying this tier are mostly buying headroom and quietness.

Runner-up: **Asetek Forte at £1,449.** 18Nm peak, smoother torque ramp than Simucube, RaceHub software is friendlier than True Drive for drivers who don't want to live in a settings menu. Asetek's pedal ecosystem (Invicta-S pedals at £1,299 separately) is the strongest at this tier and arguably the best argument to go Asetek over Simucube if you're buying base + pedals together.

Third place: **Fanatec Podium DD1 at £1,599**, 20Nm peak. The Podium ecosystem is fully realised here (rims from £400 to £1,200), software is Fanalab (still slower than Pit House and True Drive). Buy only if you've already invested in Podium rims. Skip otherwise.

Skip pick at this tier: **MOZA R21 at £1,499**. MOZA's flagship is a fine base but you're paying R21 prices for what is, fundamentally, an R12 with more headroom. The diminishing-returns curve says: if you're spending R21 money, you're spending Simucube money. Make the switch.

Who should buy: front-of-the-grid league drivers, IRL pro/semi-pro drivers using the rig for serious training, hobbyists who genuinely value quietness and software configurability and have the income for the headroom (no judgement, this is an expensive hobby and pretending otherwise is patronising).

Who should NOT: anyone who answered "because I want the best" to the question of why. The best for what. If you can't answer, you're buying signalling, not driving.

What changes at the next tier: it stops being about FFB and starts being about brand loyalty. Asetek Invicta torque numbers exist mostly to put numbers on a spec sheet that no consumer rig can transmit without bolting to a concrete floor.

Sim-specific: at this tier all bases handle every sim well. Differences become preference, not capability. Simucube and Asetek both shine on iRacing's high-frequency FFB; Fanatec edges them on F1 25 because of the licence-tied rim integration.

## £2,000+, for people who care about every Nm

**Best buy (with a wince): Asetek Invicta at £2,199.**

26Nm peak, the best pedal ecosystem in sim racing (Invicta-S brake at 200kg load cell range, the gold standard), two-year EU warranty, RaceHub software. If you're buying at this tier and you actually drive enough to use it, Invicta plus Invicta-S pedals plus a Sim-Lab P1X cockpit is the rig I'd specify. Total cost at that spec: £4,800 ish. Yes, really.

Runner-up: **Simucube 2 Ultimate at £2,499**, 32Nm peak. Same DNA as the Pro, more headroom, same software. The R&D pedigree (Simucube come from industrial-control engineering, not gaming) shows up in the noise floor and the high-frequency FFB clarity. Buy if you're a True Drive obsessive.

Skip pick at this tier: anything claiming "30Nm+" without a corresponding pedal-set, cockpit, and league-relevant use case. 32Nm peak means you need a £600+ aluminium-profile rig to not feel ridiculous, you need pedals that can match the input fidelity (Heusinkveld Ultimate+ at £999, Invicta-S at £1,299), and you need a use case that isn't "I race ACC GT3 on the weekends". Most of the hardware stack at this tier is bought as a system or it's wasted.

Who should buy: pro and semi-pro drivers, league drivers with sponsored rigs, hobbyists with explicit cost-no-object framing who want the platform stable for ten years.

Who should NOT: 95% of the readers of this site. This is the tier where the spreadsheet-of-features starts to lie. 32Nm of torque is a number; what 32Nm feels like in your spare bedroom on a Trak Racer TR8 is closer to "a bit much" than "transformative".

Sim-specific: at this tier sim choice doesn't change the recommendation. The base will handle anything. Your driving is the limit, not the gear.

## The one specific recommendation

If you came here for an answer, buy the **MOZA R9 + CS V2P rim bundle at £549 from GPL Racing**, fit Heusinkveld Sprint pedals (£525 separately), and bolt the lot to a Trak Racer TR8 (£429). Total cost: £1,503. That rig is 90% of what my £2,200 setup gives me on lap-time consistency over a 30-lap ACC GT3 stint at Spa, and it's a rig you can recommend without the wince. Anything cheaper compromises on pedals (the highest-impact upgrade in the chain). Anything more expensive is buying headroom you won't use unless you're already winning your league.

If you've already got pedals you trust, just the R9 base + bundle at £549 is the answer and you stop spending.

## Sim-specific gotchas

**ACC drivers**: the R9 sweet spot is real. The R12 marginal upgrade is for league racing only. Cold-tyre feel is the one place Fanatec edges MOZA at every tier, but the gap closes by lap two and ACC grids start with warm tyres in most online formats anyway.

**iRacing road drivers**: iRacing's FFB is more analytical than ACC's, less cinematic. High-bit encoders matter more here, which pushes the recommendation upward; the £800-£1,200 tier is more justifiable for serious iRacing drivers than for ACC drivers. Simucube 2 Pro shines on iRacing high-frequency content in a way the R12 doesn't quite match.

**iRacing oval drivers**: I don't drive ovals. I'm aware the FFB requirements are different (smoother, more sustained, less high-frequency). Defer to oval-specific reviewers, not this list.

**ACC league GT3 endurance**: the R12 over R9 starts to make sense once you're running 60+ minute stints with cold-rear-tyre management. Until then it's the same lap times.

**Rally drivers (EA WRC, Dirt Rally 2.0, RBR)**: defer to Bryn's rally take. Short version: Simagic Alpha Mini at £539 is sharper on direction-change than the R9 at the same price for rally-specifically. Don't buy a "best all-rounder" for rally without verifying it handles fast direction-changes under load. Also budget another £150-£200 for a hydraulic handbrake nobody in this list ships with.

**AMS2 specialists**: AMS2 punishes lazy throttle inputs and rewards information-rich FFB. Load-cell pedals matter more here than the wheel base. The R9 is enough; spend the next £400 on Heusinkveld Sprints, not on a base upgrade.

**F1 25 casuals**: any base in this list works. The G29 even works (because the assists carry it). Don't overspend; the limiting factor is the licence-tied physics, not the hardware.

## What's likely to change next quarter

**MOZA R9 v2 rumour**: chatter on the MOZA Discord since March about a refreshed R9 with the R12's encoder and a slightly stronger fan. Not confirmed by MOZA UK as of 2026-04-30. If you're not buying this week, wait until late summer.

**Fanatec post-acquisition pricing**: Fanatec was acquired by Endor's restructuring in late 2025 and pricing has been volatile through Q1 2026. CSL DD bundles have moved between £499 and £599 four times in three months. Spot-check before publishing.

**Simagic Alpha Evo Pro**: announced March 2026, expected to land in UK retail (GPL Racing as primary channel) Q3 2026 at £1,099 base-only. This is the Asetek Forte competitor and could reshape the £800-£1,200 tier. Worth waiting if you're shopping at that price.

**Logitech**: nothing on the public roadmap. Logitech have not iterated on the Pro Racing Wheel meaningfully since launch and there's no signal that's changing. The Pro Racing Wheel will continue to occupy the "skip" slot at £499 until Logitech price-corrects or ships a successor.

**Cammus C12**: new entrant from China at £599 for 12Nm direct drive. Software (Cammus Tuning) is rough and the UK aftercare track record is unproven. Could be the price-disruptor of late 2026 if the firmware story improves; right now it's the kind of base where the 18-month durability question is the live one. Wait six months and watch the RaceDepartment thread.

**VRS DirectForce Pro**: VRS continue to ship strong wheel bases for the iRacing-serious crowd. UK channel is direct-from-VRS only, which makes warranty and returns slower than Asetek or MOZA. £1,649 for the 30Nm version. Niche-by-design, not for this list's median reader.

R9 + CS V2P at GPL Racing, £549, that's the answer.
