If you only buy one game from this month’s BGG buzz, buy the one that matches your table reality, not the hotter thumbnail.

Right now, **Lairs** and **Bailiff of Boscoop** are pulling attention for very different reasons. Lairs looks like the cleaner pitch for groups that want immediate tension and quick turns. Bailiff looks like the stronger pick for players who enjoy economy pressure and tactical planning over multiple rounds.

The mistake is assuming “higher in Hotness” means “better for your group”. It doesn’t. Hotness measures conversation and momentum, not long-term table fit.

## Fast verdict

- Choose **Lairs** if your group wants faster onboarding and lower rules overhead.
- Choose **Bailiff of Boscoop** if your group likes tighter optimisation puzzles and can tolerate a slower first teach.
- **Skip both for now** if your group is already shelf-of-shame heavy and you still haven’t landed your current medium-weight euro.

## What we know from April trend signals

From current April chatter and BGG Hotness movement:

- **Lairs, Bailiff of Boscoop, and Eternal Decks** are repeatedly showing up in “what should I buy next?” discussions.
- Most early excitement is centred on novelty and mechanics pitch, not on “after 10 plays” durability.
- Groups are split between “instant table hit” and “slow-burn strategy game” preferences.

That split matters more than rank position.

## Lairs: best for momentum tables

### Best for

- 3–4 players who value pace over deep optimisation
- Weeknight groups that want one clean teach and a full game inside ~90 minutes
- Mixed-skill tables where one person usually teaches everyone else

### Skip if

- Your group expects heavy strategic arc and long-horizon planning
- You dislike light randomness in tactical moments
- You want a game that rewards deep meta study from play one

### Trade-off you need to accept

Lairs appears to trade some strategic granularity for speed and accessibility. That can be exactly right for real-world groups, but it also means high-level players may solve core lines faster than they’d like.

### Hidden friction

Quick-teach games often feel great in session one, then flatten by session five if your group values depth. If your table is mostly “hard euro” players, test before buying blind.

## Bailiff of Boscoop: best for thinky groups

### Best for

- 2–4 players who enjoy layered decision trees
- Groups that like tension from tight economies and timing windows
- Players who are happy spending turn one and two setting up engine rhythm

### Skip if

- Your table gets impatient with deliberate pacing
- You often play with newcomers who hate rules overhead
- You want a breezy “arrive and play” experience

### Trade-off you need to accept

Bailiff looks like the stronger long-term puzzle, but the cost is a heavier first play. The teach burden is real, especially if one person is carrying setup, rules explanation, and edge-case arbitration.

### Hidden friction

At 4 players, downtime risk can rise if your group analysis-paralysis tendencies are already high. If your table frequently stalls in planning phases, this may become “admire not play”.

## Who should skip both this month

Skip both if any of these are true:

- You already own 2+ unplayed medium-weight strategy games
- Your regular group rarely meets at full count
- You’re buying from hype and not from a specific table slot

A better use of budget: upgrade an existing favourite with insert/storage, sleeves for a highly replayed game, or one expansion that fixes known pain points in your current rotation.

## UK buying plan (practical)

Treat this as a staged buy, not a panic buy.

1. **Check UK Amazon listing stability first** (price + stock volatility over 7 days).
2. **Check eBay sold listings** for realistic second-hand floor and demand.
3. Buy only when one of these happens:
   - Price lands inside your target band, or
   - You have a confirmed game-night slot for it within two weeks.

That simple gate prevents shelf drift.

## Final recommendation

- If your group is casual-competitive and plays after work: **Lairs** is the safer immediate buy.
- If your group enjoys denser euro-style tension and can absorb a slower teach: **Bailiff of Boscoop** is the stronger long-run buy.

Hotness is useful for discovery, not decision quality. Use it to build a shortlist, then buy for your table’s habits, patience, and replay appetite.
