Games Workshop’s Armageddon week gave us exactly the kind of release cycle that empties hobby budgets fast:

- New **Space Marine detachment** momentum
- New **Ork detachment** momentum via Speedwaaagh
- A **Chaplain with Jump Pack** reveal in the same news window

If you felt the urge to panic-buy, that’s normal. But the smart move is still the boring one: buy for table function, not headline excitement.

## Fast Verdict

If you only read one section:

- **Space Marines:** Buy the new character/support piece only if your current list already runs jump pressure and you know the slot it replaces.
- **Orks:** Buy broad-use core units before detachment-specific spice.
- **Everyone else:** This is a planning week, not a checkout week.

## Why This Week Feels Different (and Why It Mostly Isn’t)

Armageddon content is doing what good GW narrative beats always do: it makes faction identity feel urgent. That’s fun. It also distorts purchasing logic.

TabletopWatch’s internal stack is healthy right now (latest TabletopWatch test: **3,444 products**, fresh listings, and active price tracking), which means there are plenty of options in market. Availability is not your bottleneck. Decision quality is.

Most games are still decided by:

1. Reliable scoring pieces
2. Trading efficiency
3. Movement and board control

Detachment tricks matter, but they usually matter *after* your fundamentals are stable.

## Space Marines: New Detachment Energy + Jump Pack Chaplain Reveal

### Best for

- Players already on a jump-heavy or pressure shell
- Players with a clear event list and tested mission plan
- Players replacing an underperforming support slot (not adding random complexity)

### Skip if

- You still miss basic scoring bodies
- You’re building your first stable 2,000-point core
- You’re buying because “new model, new power” rather than “clear role upgrade”

### Trade-off to be honest about

The upside is obvious: better pressure sequencing and list identity.

The downside: once you commit to more specialized support interactions, your list can become less forgiving in casual mixed matchups. If your local meta is inconsistent, hyper-tuned synergy can underperform compared to a simpler all-comers shell.

## Orks: Speedwaaagh Momentum Without Budget Burn

Ork reveal weeks are where smart players gain edge and everyone else accumulates grey plastic they don’t finish.

### Best for

- Established Ork players who already own a playable backbone
- Players who know whether they want pressure, attrition, or mission denial first

### Skip if

- You’re still missing core objective play
- You have a painting backlog longer than your next six game nights
- You can’t explain what role each new purchase fills in your next three lists

### Trade-off to be honest about

Detachment-led buying feels efficient because it looks “meta aware.” In practice, it can trap you into narrow list branches that get old quickly if your group rotates terrain, missions, and opponents.

A wider, reusable core gives you more replay value and fewer regret purchases.

## The Buy Order That Usually Works

Use this sequence and you’ll avoid most reveal-week mistakes:

1. **Core mission piece** (scoring/board presence)
2. **One role-fixing support purchase** (delivery, buff, or durability fix)
3. **Only then** add the headline reveal unit/detachment spice

If you reverse this, you often end up with:

- Better-looking list screenshots
- Worse real table consistency

## A Concrete Table Scenario

You’re on a five-objective mission, round 3, against a player who trades efficiently in the midfield.

If your reveal-week spend went into flashy synergy but you skipped durable scoring bodies, you’ll feel great in one combat phase and then run out of pieces to hold primary.

If your spend went fundamentals first, you’ll usually still have enough board to close the game even when the spicy interaction whiffs once.

That is the difference between hype buying and win-rate buying.

## Price Reality Check (Before You Commit)

TabletopWatch deal data this week is deep enough to compare properly (multiple retailer sources live, fresh scrape cadence, tracked history). Use that to your advantage:

- Don’t buy day-one if your list doesn’t need it now
- Watch for a short pullback once reveal heat cools
- Prioritize purchases that remain useful across at least two detachment directions

A good deal on the wrong unit is still a bad buy.

## Who Should Skip This Entire Cycle

You should skip this reveal cycle entirely if:

- You haven’t played enough recent games to identify your real weak point
- You’re still painting last month’s “must buy”
- You’re learning core mission play and need reps, not new complexity

The most underrated upgrade in 40K is not a new release. It’s 6–10 games with one coherent list.

## Final Recommendation

Armageddon week is strong for ideas, not always strong for immediate purchases.

If you’re a tuned Space Marine or Ork player with a known list gap, there are real upgrade opportunities here.

If not, bank the budget, play more reps, and buy one month later with better information.

That sounds less exciting. It usually wins more games and saves more money.
