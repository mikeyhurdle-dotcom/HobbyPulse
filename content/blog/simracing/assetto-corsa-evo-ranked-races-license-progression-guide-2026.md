AC EVO’s ranked-race push is creating the same mistake loop we always see: people buy torque before fixing consistency.

If your goal is license progression, **your brake repeatability and rig stability matter more than headline wheelbase specs**.

## Quick verdict

- **Upgrade pedals first** if your trail-braking is inconsistent.
- **Upgrade rig stiffness second** if load-cell braking moves your seating position or pedal angle.
- **Upgrade wheelbase later** when your braking trace is already stable.

## Why this matters now

RaceDepartment’s latest AC EVO coverage is centered on a proper ranked-race and license progression structure. That changes incentives:

- one-off fast laps matter less,
- repeatable race pace matters more,
- errors under pressure get punished harder.

That means your hardware decisions should prioritize consistency under load, not peak feel in a five-lap test.

## The practical upgrade order for most drivers

## 1) Pedals (first)

### Best for
- Drivers missing braking points by small margins
- Drivers with inconsistent release timing into medium-speed corners

### Skip if
- Your current pedals are already consistent and your misses are mostly racecraft decisions

### Trade-off / caveat
A better pedal set improves signal quality, but it also exposes poor braking habits quickly. If you don’t recalibrate technique, you won’t unlock the gain.

## 2) Rig stiffness (second)

### Best for
- Anyone feeling rig flex under heavy braking
- Drivers whose seating or pedal angle shifts between laps

### Who should skip
- Drivers on already-rigid profiles with no measurable movement

### Trade-off / caveat
Rig upgrades feel boring compared with a new wheelbase, but they remove hidden variability. The downside is obvious: less excitement per pound spent.

## 3) Wheelbase jump (third)

### Best for
- Drivers with stable braking traces who need finer FFB detail for edge cases
- League drivers chasing consistency at higher pace ceilings

### Skip if
- You’re still correcting basic entry mistakes and over-driving corner phase one

### Trade-off / caveat
More torque/headroom gives better information, but poor gain setup can create clipping, fatigue, and worse long-run performance.

## Concrete scenario

You’re in AC EVO ranked sprint races and keep losing rating in the same way:

- Lap 1–2: decent pace
- Mid-race: one braking lockup or under-rotation at a heavy stop
- Final laps: over-correction and confidence drop

That pattern is usually brake consistency + rig stability, not wheelbase peak torque.

Fix those first and your license progression improves faster.

## Validation checks after each change

Run this before you call any upgrade “worth it”:

- 10-lap run, same fuel and track state
- Compare braking-point variance at 2 heavy braking zones
- Check whether minimum corner speed is more repeatable
- Track incident count over 3 ranked sessions

If none of that improves, the change didn’t solve your bottleneck.

## Who should skip upgrades entirely right now

Skip spending this month if:

- you’re switching car/track combo every session,
- you have no baseline telemetry or replay routine,
- your biggest issue is racecraft decisions, not control consistency.

Do two weeks of repeat sessions with one combo first.
